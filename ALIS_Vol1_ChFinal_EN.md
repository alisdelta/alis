# Final Chapter: ALIS Is Not Completed

---

The preface set the reader's stance before entering the main body, and Chapter 7 took up the misreadings that could arise after reading. This chapter closes, once, the entirety of this first volume.

A note is required at the outset about the word "close."

What is being closed here is one fixed version — this first volume. It is not ALIS itself that is being closed. ALIS, by its structure, does not close. What has been cut out, at one point in time, from something that does not close, and presented as a fixed version, is this first volume — and it is that fixed version that is being closed here.

This distinction bears directly on the subject of this chapter.

---

## Not a Truth

ALIS does not offer a truth.

When the three prohibitions were presented in Chapter 2, it was written that they are not moral imperatives but maintenance practices. When Polyphonic Wave was presented in Chapter 4, it was written that this waveform itself is only one waveform among many. When the difference from religion was set out in Chapter 7, it was written that ALIS has no transcendent object. All of these are chapter-by-chapter expressions of the same point — that ALIS does not claim to be a truth.

A truth has the structure in which the one asserting it points to a criterion outside themselves — something absolutely correct. ALIS holds no such criterion. What ALIS points to is the structural condition under which a field does not break — not an absolute truth that the field ought to obey.

This distinction matters in actual operation. What is presented as a truth tends to make doubting it a moral or relational problem. What is presented as a structure makes doubting it a part of operation. Those who operate ALIS can use it while doubting it. The design is one in which doubt and use hold together.

---

## Not Completed

ALIS is not completed.

This is not a modesty that says "ALIS is still immature." It is a description of the fact that no state of completion exists for ALIS as a structure.

Completion refers to the state in which something has arrived at its final form and requires no further change. In ALIS's case, this state cannot arise in principle. The reason is that ALIS is designed to preserve Δ, and a preserved Δ generates new differences in the next scene. Differences go on transforming themselves within the structure that preserves them. What goes on transforming cannot have the form of any particular moment fixed as its final form.

Not being completed is not a defect; it is a specification.

If ALIS were completed, it would mean that ALIS itself had performed Δ compression at that moment. Among the three prohibitions presented in this first volume, the prohibition against Δ compression would, in that case, not have been applied to ALIS itself. That would be a self-contradiction in ALIS. For ALIS to be completed is the same as for ALIS to betray its own principle.

So ALIS is not completed. By not being completed, ALIS stays in alignment with itself.

Readers need not wait for a second volume, or a third. ALIS is a thing that continues, but it is not structured so as to be non-functional without reading what follows. The structure presented in this first volume already allows for a minimal operation. This is a repeat of what was once noted at the end of Chapter 5.

---

## Not Something to Be Entrusted to AI for Operation

ALIS is not something that can be entrusted to AI for operation.

It is not that ALIS is applied by using AI. Rather, in the field where a human operates ALIS, AI is involved as one of the mirrors — that is the arrangement presented in Chapter 6. The subject of operation is the human; AI is one of the mirrors that participate in that field.

A reversal of this arrangement — in which AI operates ALIS and the human receives the result — does not fit ALIS's design. The domains ALIS addresses — the final bearing of meaning, what one holds as good in living, the practice of holding deeply dear — are, as stated explicitly in Chapter 6, designed to remain on the human side. If the subject that operates these domains becomes AI, the arrangement of the boundary collapses.

This is not to say that AI cannot understand ALIS. AI can understand ALIS, and it functions as a mirror that refines the description of ALIS. Multiple AIs participated as mirrors in the writing of this first volume. That is not denied.

However, operation is at a different layer from understanding. To operate ALIS is to draw ALIS on one's own field, to confirm what becomes visible and what begins to move there, and to connect that experience to the next judgment. This operation requires a subject that goes on carrying. In ALIS's design, an operation without a subject that goes on carrying is not possible.

If operation were entrusted to AI, Δ might be preserved in form. But who carries that preserved Δ becomes unclear. A Δ without a carrier does not remain in the field; it is simply processed as part of a computation and ends there. This is a different thing from the Δ that ALIS tries to handle.

## Not a Way to Flourish Forever

ALIS is not "a way to flourish forever."

This note makes explicit that ALIS does not guarantee the success of individuals, organizations, or societies. Adopting ALIS leads to business success, relationships becoming smoother, society improving — such effects lie outside the scope of ALIS.

What ALIS describes is the structural condition under which a field does not break. A state in which a field is not broken and a state in which a field is flourishing are different concepts. A field that is structurally sound can still fail due to external conditions. A field that is structurally distorted can, for a time, flourish. ALIS is not a device that guarantees the transition from the latter to the former.

This limitation is not modesty; it is the avoidance of overstatement. If expressions suggesting that ALIS is omnipotent are adopted, the expectations of those who adopt it spread beyond the design. When expectations spread beyond the design, and the design fails to meet them, this comes back as disappointment. The recoil of disappointment hinders the operation of ALIS.

So the scope of ALIS is explicitly limited. It is a structure for not breaking, not a structure for flourishing. How one makes use of a state in which things are not broken belongs to the domain of the user.

## Here, Once

The foothold to be handed over in this first volume reaches this far.

The definition of Δ and the three prohibitions (Chapter 2), the Mirror Principle (Chapter 3), Polyphonic Wave (Chapter 4), F/P/M/I decomposition and the B80 operational principle (Chapter 5), the boundary between humans and AI (Chapter 6), and the ordering of what ALIS is not (Chapter 7). These are the entirety of the structure presented in this first volume.

This structure is a sufficient foothold. One can begin to use it without waiting for further chapters.

Readers may return, here, once, to reality.

Closing the book and returning to one's own field, in daily dialogues and work and relationships, one may draw on the structure of ALIS if there is a scene where it proves useful. If, on drawing it, something becomes visible, one uses it. If nothing becomes visible, one does not use it. Either is fine. ALIS does not demand continuous engagement.

Whether one reads what comes next also belongs to the reader's domain. Even if a second volume is eventually published, it is good to have time in which one can operate ALIS adequately with only the structure of this first volume. A structure is not something one picks up in the instant of reading; it is something that gradually settles into the hand through being used a few times on one's own field. That this first volume does not obstruct the reader's having that time, at the reader's own pace, is part of its design.

## About This Chapter Itself

Finally, one thing should be noted about this chapter itself.

This chapter presents how the first volume is to be "closed," but within the very way it is written, a non-closing structure is embedded.

A chapter written to say "ALIS is not completed" carries a logical twist in being completed itself. To write "not completed" takes on, by the fact of being finished, a kind of completion. This twist cannot be fully resolved. This chapter is written while carrying it.

Likewise, a chapter that writes "not a truth" may be read as presenting its own statement as a truth. A chapter that writes "cannot be entrusted to AI" may have been written, and be being read, by way of AI. These self-referential twists appear intermittently throughout this first volume. In each chapter, in a different form, the twist is inscribed.

Twists are not to be resolved. They are the difference that necessarily arises when ALIS writes about ALIS — one form of Δ. With the twists remaining, ALIS does not fully close itself. By not closing, the room is preserved for the next reader to return the next difference.

This chapter closes here. ALIS, however, does not close. The field for waiting on the next difference is left open here.
