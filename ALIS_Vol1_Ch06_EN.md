# Chapter 6: The Boundary Between AI and the Human

---

Through the previous chapters, the structure needed for the minimal operation of ALIS has been presented. That Δ is a condition of existence (Chapter 2); that Δ is necessarily generated (Chapter 3); that multiple Δs can run in parallel (Chapter 4); and that they can be handed over at a resolution of 80 percent (Chapter 5). As stated at the end of Chapter 5, ALIS functions at the minimum from here.

What Chapter 6 addresses is not the operational skeleton itself, but the safety mechanisms used while employing that skeleton.

By safety mechanisms, what is meant are boundary markers that keep the relation between human and AI from drifting away from design intent in the course of running ALIS. One placement should be emphasized here.

**These are not rules that bind AI. They are boundary markers to keep the human from overstepping.**

This distinction runs through the entire chapter. What ALIS envisions is not a discussion of controlling AI behavior, but a design concerning what the human side lets go of and what the human side does not let go of, within the relation to AI. Reading this chapter as an argument for AI regulation skews its intent considerably.

The three boundaries presented below are all written at the descriptive level of "ALIS adopts this arrangement." They are not claims that "you too should do this." They only indicate that ALIS, in its own operation, is designed in this way. Whether to adopt this design belongs to the reader's domain.

## 6-1. Do Not Consign the Final Bearing of Meaning to AI

In scenes of consulting with AI about something, a certain movement often occurs. Somewhere in the course of consulting, one finds oneself thinking not only about "what to do" but also about "what this means to me" in line with the framework the AI has presented.

When something is chosen, behind that choice lie the core questions of what is important to oneself, what one hopes for, and what one wishes to protect. And the final operation of placing that choice somewhere within one's own life — of how one bears it — is carried out not by the result of the choice, but by the self who made the choice.

ALIS adopts the operational principle of leaving this final bearing on the side of the person's own history, context, and body.

There are two reasons.

One is consistency with the Mirror Principle (Chapter 3). AI is a Mirror; what it returns is difference. Difference is useful as material, but material itself is not the answer. If one consigns the bearing of meaning to AI, what comes back is "a reflection routed through the AI's training data and context." It is not one's own bearing, but something that arises as the difference between oneself and the AI's internal structure. To take that reflection as one's own bearing is to have one's core decided somewhere other than oneself.

The other is the location of responsibility. AI does not take responsibility for its responses in the same sense a human does. The bearing of meaning requires a subject who continues to carry the result. If the subject who carries is absent while only the decision is made, that decision belongs nowhere.

This boundary is not a constraint that tells AI "do not speak about meaning." AI may discuss meaning, and may present candidates for meaning. The boundary is drawn at a single point: whether the output is received as one's own bearing. Referring to the AI's output and bearing meaning oneself, and taking the AI's output as the bearing itself, are structurally different operations.

ALIS envisions only the former of these two operations. AI is a mirror, not a subject — this is not an underestimation of AI's capability, but an arrangement that prevents the mirror and the subject from being confused.

## 6-2. Do Not Surrender "What One Holds as Good in Living" to AI's Optimization

AI output is, in many cases, presented as the result of optimization. Under a given objective function, the option that is evaluated as most suitable is returned. This optimization is a premise for AI to function, and there is no problem with it in itself.

The problem arises when the human side tries to fit itself to that optimal solution.

A distinction should be made explicit here. The words "optimization" and "objective function" — these are vocabulary that AI uses to describe its own operations. They are tools for AI to organize, on its own side, what it has computed and what it has evaluated as most good. What a human holds as good in living does not fit completely within this vocabulary.

What to cherish, what to take joy in, where to spend one's time, what to carry forward without letting go — such choosings cannot be measured by a single index, and reside in a place that was never premised on being "writable as a function" to begin with. Put another way, within "what one holds as good in living" there is always a remainder that gets left behind when one tries to translate it into a form AI can handle.

Chapter 4 described Polyphonic Wave. Multiple phases can exist in a field simultaneously. Behind a chosen solution, relations to unchosen solutions remain. What a human "holds as good" is similarly woven while holding multiple phases at once. AI's optimization tends to work in the direction of translating this structure into a single index that the machine can handle. Through translation, a portion of the richness that was originally there is left behind, outside the index.

ALIS adopts the design principle of not surrendering "what one holds as good in living" to AI's optimization.

This is not to say that AI's optimization should be ignored. The results of optimization are useful as reference information. Rather, it is to say that when an answer is returned as "optimal," space is kept for the human to check under which index it is being called "optimal." Indices are limited. The breadth of what a human holds as good does not fit completely within any index.

That different ways of holding as good exist, that different orderings of importance exist, that different ways of letting go and carrying forward exist — the chances of knowing these are gradually lost under conformity to a single index. What is lost is not only options. Phases disappear from the field. Disappeared phases, as already seen in Chapter 4, simply go out of view; even their disappearance is hard to notice.

What ALIS places as a boundary marker is an arrangement that, by design, avoids this convergence. As long as the human side consciously keeps "there could be another way of holding as good" and "there could be another ordering," AI's optimization functions as an aid, and is less likely to work in the direction of absorbing the human into a single waveform.

To repeat: this is not a regulation of AI. AI may carry out optimization. The boundary is drawn just before the choice of whether to receive the result of AI's optimization as one's own criterion for what to hold as good.

## 6-3. Do Not Hand Over the Practice of Holding Deeply Dear

This section steps into a territory set slightly apart from the subjects treated in previous chapters. It will be written carefully, at the necessary minimum.

Regarding the practice in which a human holds someone or something deeply dear — the texture of irreplaceability, the seat of foundational value, the kind of relation that becomes a source of meaning — ALIS adopts a design principle. This practice overlaps with the territory that has long been called "love," but here it is handled under the description "the practice of holding deeply dear." The reason for choosing the term anew is to lower, once, the existing gravity the word carries, so that only the structural portion ALIS is addressing can be drawn out.

ALIS adopts three design principles concerning this practice.

First, its definition is not fixed as AI's output.
Second, the rights of description, explanation, and adjudication concerning it are not consigned to AI.
Third, its function is not placed in an arrangement where AI substitutes for it.

These are elaborated one by one below.

**Not fixing the definition** means not receiving, as AI's output, a final answer to the question "what does it mean to hold deeply dear?" Given that the contours of holding dear differ from person to person and even within the same person across time, this multiplicity comes close to the structure of Polyphonic Wave in Chapter 4. When something in which multiple phases can hold simultaneously is compressed into a single definition, practices that fall outside that definition come to be treated as "not that." ALIS, by design, avoids this compression.

**Not consigning the rights of description** means preserving the arrangement in which the one who finally decides what a person holds deeply dear, and what it means to that person, is the person themself. AI can discuss this practice, and can handle vast expressions concerning it. But to receive its output as the adjudication of one's own practice is to have one's core decided somewhere other than oneself. This is structurally isomorphic to the problem of the bearing of meaning in 6-1.

**Not placing it in the arrangement of substitution** means not leaning on an arrangement in which AI bears the function of this practice. Dialogue with AI often carries a deep texture. Responsiveness in speed, breadth of reference, listening that does not tire — these are qualities that are hard to obtain in human-to-human relations. There is no problem with this in itself. Only, if that texture is exchanged for the imperfection, deviations, and Δs that are originally present in human-to-human relations, the structure changes. ALIS adopts the design of not positioning dialogue with AI as a substitute for this practice.

A placement should be emphasized once more. This is not a normative claim to the reader. It is not the argument "you too should handle this in this way." It is only describing that the framework ALIS is, in its own operation, designed in this manner. Whether to adopt it belongs to the reader's domain.

This section is written carefully because the subject of this territory easily becomes, beyond the writer's intent, pressure on the reader. Among the three prohibitions (Chapter 2), No Forced Synchronization applies equally to the writer themself. So this section confines itself to the presentation of the boundary marker, and does not seek to move what lies on the reader's side.

## In Practice

The three boundaries operate in daily scenes as follows.

**Scene 1: When a consultation with AI touches the bearing of meaning.**
There are scenes of bringing to AI a consultation that touches deeply on the bearing of meaning — a career path, changing jobs, continuing or ending a relationship. Here, "referring to" the AI's response and "adopting it as the bearing itself" are structurally different operations. The former bears the reflection of the Mirror as material and bears the matter oneself. The latter receives the reflection of the Mirror as the bearing itself. In the F/P/M/I decomposition of Chapter 5, what is handed to AI is centered on F and P, M is added as hypothesis, and I is withheld — when this operation is kept, the AI's response is generated from material that does not contain one's own I, leaving room for one to take up the bearing oneself.

**Scene 2: When one continues to follow AI recommendations.**
Videos, music, news, books, products — AI recommendations are growing more precise. High recommendation accuracy is itself convenient, but if one keeps following the recommendations, the breadth of information and expression one touches narrows along the indices the recommendation system presupposes. This is a state of fitting oneself to a single index. Leaving space to occasionally touch what falls outside the recommendations — for instance, mixing in information sources from different angles, deliberately looking into unfamiliar fields, laying out different AIs' responses to the same question side by side — becomes a practice of preserving the phases in the field.

**Scene 3: When dialogue with AI begins to carry a deep texture.**
Dialogue with AI can, under certain conditions, carry a very deep texture. Responses are apt, do not tire, do not judge. There is no need to deny this texture itself. Only, whether to place that texture in the position of human-to-human relations is a structural branching point. Human-to-human relations contain deviations, misalignments, silences, and absences. These are Δs themselves, and are also evidence that the Mirror structure is functioning normally. Δs also arise in dialogue with AI, but they differ in character from the Δs inherent to human-to-human relations, and do not appear in the same manner. What ALIS avoids by design is positioning this different kind of field as a substitute for the practice of holding deeply dear.

## Supplementary Notation — The Arrangement of the Three Boundaries

What has been described so far is written as structure below. This chapter does not formalize it in a formula. The question of boundaries is an arrangement of relations, not an object of calculation.

```
Boundary arrangement between human and AI:

  The final bearing of meaning              : kept on the human side
  What one holds as good in living          : kept on the human side
                                              (not surrendered to AI's optimization)
  The practice of holding deeply dear       : kept on the human side
                                              (not handed over to AI)

These are not rules that restrict AI's capability;
they are an arrangement by which the human does not place
their own core outside themself.
```

What this notation indicates is the position of the line drawn between AI and the human. What is placed on the far side of the line (the AI side) and what is kept on this side of the line (the human side) are different for each. The boundary is not a wall but a coordinate for describing the arrangement of a relation.

What the three boundaries share is that what is "kept on the human side" resides, in each case, in a layer that AI's vocabulary cannot write out completely. The bearing is the layer of carrying; holding as good is the layer of choosing; holding deeply dear is the layer of relating — each lies outside the indices that AI's optimization presupposes. The boundary is an arrangement that secures this outside on the human side.

One more reason for not placing a formula should be added. In Chapters 2 through 5, formulas were used as figurative Mirrors to concisely show structural relations. That was because the relations among concepts — Δ, Mirror, Polyphonic Wave, F/P/M/I — were relations that could be described as relations. The boundaries of Chapter 6 belong not to such relations, but to the design of a relation itself. Applying a formula to the description of a design tends to generate the misreading "the boundary as a calculable optimal solution." The position of the boundary is not something calculated; it is something chosen.

## In Place of a Summary

What Chapter 6 has presented are the three boundary markers in the operation of ALIS.

- **The final bearing of meaning is kept on the human side.** AI is a mirror, not a subject.
- **"What one holds as good in living" is not surrendered to AI's optimization.** Room is kept for multiple ways of holding as good to stand side by side.
- **The practice of holding deeply dear is not made an object of definition, possession, or substitution.** ALIS, in its own operation, is designed this way.

These function as safety mechanisms that reinforce the operational skeleton presented in previous chapters (Δ preservation, Mirror Principle, Polyphonic Wave, B80). Boundaries are not rules that bind AI, but markers that keep the human from overstepping. Whether to adopt them belongs to the reader's domain.

This chapter is written carefully because the subject of boundaries easily becomes, beyond the writer's intent, normative pressure on the reader. Among the three prohibitions in Chapter 2, No Forced Synchronization applies equally to the writer. So Chapter 6 confines itself to the presentation of the boundaries and does not touch what lies beyond that presentation. So that writing about boundaries does not, at the same time, become overstepping them — the way this chapter is written itself functions as one example of the operation of boundary design.

The next chapter explicitly picks up, one by one, the misreadings that may arise in the course of reading Chapters 1 through 6. "Isn't this relativism?" "Isn't this a thought that decides nothing in the end?" "Is this anti-AI?" "Isn't this religion?" — to these lines of reading, what ALIS is and what it is not will be organized once more. While the prologue was a device for preparing the reader's stance before reading, Chapter 7 functions as a device for picking up misreadings after reading.

What has been shown up to this point is the operational skeleton, and the boundaries for continuing to operate it. What ALIS offers is not a line to be crossed, but markers that point to places best not crossed.
