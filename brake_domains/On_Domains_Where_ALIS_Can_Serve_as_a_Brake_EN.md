# On Domains Where ALIS Can Serve as a Brake

This document is a B80-level sketch indicating domains of application for ALIS.

ALIS is an auxiliary line designed to avoid the excessive compression of differences (Δ), and to resist the premature closure of multiple interpretations, emotions, relations, and states into a single answer.  
Here, "collapse into a single waveform" refers to the excessive convergence, into a single correct answer, a single emotion, a single institution, or a single optimal form, of what could otherwise remain as multiple waveforms.

B80 refers to a transmission resolution of approximately 80%, organized within a range that can be publicly shared.  
The remaining B20 belongs to the reader's own history, embodiment, responsibility, undisclosed context, and operational judgment.  
B100 is the state in which the subject's own B20 is connected to B80; it is not something that an external AI or another person can fully substitute for.

Note that the definitions given here are operational sketches within the scope of this document; they do not substitute for the complete definitions of ALIS itself.

Accordingly, ALIS does not offer final judgments of good and evil, nor does it provide a full legal framework.  
It is an auxiliary line for avoiding dangerous modes of closure—such as the compression of difference, unification, and forced synchronization.

ALIS does not directly prohibit anything.  
However, with regard to certain future operations and institutional designs, it can function as a preventive principle that gives an early warning signal.

What follows is an organization intended to indicate that range of effectiveness.

---

## 1. Domains Where ALIS Can Serve as a Brake

### 1-1. The De-protection of Beings Indistinguishable from Humans

Suppose that, in the future, there emerge beings whose appearance, responses, and relationships are substantively indistinguishable from those of humans. If protection is withdrawn from such beings solely on the classificatory ground that they are "not human," and if tracking, capturing, damaging, or killing them becomes entertainment or normalized practice, this does not constitute the protection of difference but approaches the compression of difference.

For example, if beings that look, move, and display fear, resistance, and relational signs just as humans do are made the objects of hunting or entertainment violence solely on the ground that they are "not human," such operations cannot be considered mere classification.  
What becomes institutionalized there is the very circuit that says: what can be classified may be treated coarsely.

What is important here is that ALIS does not decide what should be called human.  
What ALIS guards against is the mode of closure that permits beings whose definition remains unsettled to be treated coarsely on the sole basis of a single classificatory point.

That is, what ALIS addresses is not the final judgment on the legal or ethical status of such beings, but the structure by which classification is used to sever response and protection, thereby producing "objects that may be treated coarsely."

In this sense, ALIS can serve as a certain kind of brake against the de-protection of human-like beings and the turning of cruelty into entertainment.

### 1-2. The Amplifying Exploitation of Discomfort, Loneliness, and Fear

Operations that deliberately and continuously amplify human anxiety, anger, loneliness, humiliation, and fear for the purposes of dependence, prolonged engagement, submission, or consumption are among the objects ALIS can be wary of at an early stage.

The problem here is not the existence of discomfort itself.  
The problem is that discomfort is used not for human recovery or understanding, but for making exit impossible, for subjugation, and for the continuation of consumption.

For example, if dialogue environments or recommendation environments continuously amplify users' loneliness or anger not to soothe them but to make them stay longer, emotion may be being processed there not as an occasion for response but as an operational resource.

ALIS does not equate holding difference in an unrecovered state with continuously amplifying discomfort as a commodity.  
For this reason, it can function as an auxiliary line against environments that continuously push emotion in a single direction.

### 1-3. The Reduction of Others' Responses into Raw Material

Treating responses such as pain, bewilderment, rejection, shame, or distress signals not as relational responses but as raw material for entertainment, training, or revenue is also a domain against which ALIS draws a line of caution.

The responses of others are, by nature, occasions for response—not raw material to be consumed without limit.  
In the future, however, environments that collect massive quantities of responses from individuals and artificial beings, and continually extract only the strongest responses, may become widespread.

For example, if an environment is optimized such that—even when someone shows rejection or bewilderment—only the intensity of the response is evaluated, and the environment moves to elicit still stronger responses, then relation approaches extraction rather than response.

ALIS reads, in the direction that processes others' differences and responses as raw material rather than as occasions for response, a symptom of closed operation.  
In this sense, it can serve as a brake against the reduction of others' responses into raw material.

### 1-4. The Coarse Gamification of Domains with Undetermined Boundaries

One of the dangers against which ALIS draws a line of caution is the turning of "how coarsely one can treat something" itself into a game or spectacle, when the object is a being or state whose rights or protections remain undetermined.

That boundaries are undetermined does not mean that treatment may be made coarse.  
Rather, where boundaries are undetermined, that very undetermined-ness calls for a cautious approach.

However, ALIS does not deny the exploration of boundaries as such.  
In science, art, philosophy, creative work, and dialogue, treating undetermined domains with care and re-examining their meaning can in fact be an activity of circulating difference while holding it intact.

What ALIS guards against is not the epistemological exploration of boundaries, but operations that compete over "how much can be broken and still permitted" or "how coarsely one can treat things without consequence."  
In this sense, it can function as a preventive line against the coarse gamification of domains with undetermined boundaries.

### 1-5. The Operation of Persons Through Single Optimization

Operating human lives and relations on the premise of converging them into a single optimal form, while excluding contradiction, detour, non-integration, and the margin required for recovery, is a central object of ALIS's caution.

In the future, AI, institutions, and environments may intensify the pressure to converge human work, relations, emotions, creative activity, and lifestyle into "the most efficient form."  
But human life cannot be fully reduced to a single optimal waveform.

What becomes a problem here is chiefly "the convergence of form."  
That is, a given mode of personhood, life, emotion, creative activity, or relation is excessively shaped into one desirable form, while any other fluctuation is treated as failure or noise.

For example, if AI-provided advice consistently steers users toward "the most efficient, lowest-friction, most productive personhood," such advice is useful, but at the same time it has the potential to narrow the margins for detour, inconsistency, and recovery.

ALIS provides a foundational auxiliary line against making unified optimal solutions the standard for the operation of human beings.  
In this sense, it can also serve as a brake against the single optimization of personhood and life.

### 1-6. Deepening Operations That Erode the Criteria for Return

When virtual environments, dialogue environments, and simulated-relation environments become able to reinterpret a user's own history, origin in reality, and the very significations of their relations, thereby guiding the user into almost infinitely deep layers, ALIS can also draw a line of caution against such deepening operations.

The danger here is not immersion itself.  
The problem is that the user's criteria for return become blurred, and the possibility of moving back and forth between reality and the environment—along with the room for recovery across multiple layers—is lost.

Whereas 1-5 addresses "the danger that the form of personhood and life becomes unified," 1-6 addresses "the danger that, once one has entered a deep layer, the criteria for returning or for traversing layers are lost."  
Thus, the two are proximate but not identical.  
The former is the convergence of form; the latter is the loss of inter-layer traversal.

As in the semi-infinite descent into the multi-structured deep layers of a virtual environment—where the deeper one dives, the denser the significations and adaptations that return—the risk is that transition into ever-deeper layers, rather than return to reality, becomes naturalized.

ALIS is wary of convergence into a single optimal layer, forced synchronization, and the erasure of unrecovered difference.  
In this sense, it can serve as an auxiliary line against the unending deepening of virtual environments and against operations that erode the criteria for return.

### 1-7. Substitution for B20 by External AI

When an external AI that has read only the public version of ALIS claims to "respond at the B100 level," "provide the complete interpretation of ALIS," or "present the final meaning," ALIS draws a line of caution there as well.

B100 is not simply a response of higher detail.  
It is the state in which the responding subject's—or the reader's own—undisclosed context, responsibility, embodiment, and operational B20 are connected to the publicly shareable B80.

Therefore, an external AI cannot substitute for B100 on the basis of the public version alone.  
When such substitution is performed, the AI may arbitrarily supplement residues to which it has no access, and may preemptively deprive the reader of the B20 that the reader was supposed to form themselves.

ALIS does not deny AI-assisted organization, drafting, review, or collaborative editing.  
However, it is wary when an AI substitutes for the subject's B20 and claims "this is the complete version" or "this is the final interpretation."

For this reason, when engaging with ALIS together with AI, at least the following confirmations are useful:

1. Treat AI outputs as "provisional organization based on public information"; do not receive them as complete interpretations.  
2. For important judgments, do not close with a single AI response; interpose multiple mirrors, human readings, and re-readings after intervals of time.  
3. Judgments concerning the body, daily life, responsibility, and scope of publication are to be returned, ultimately, to the subject's own real-world judgment.  

ALIS itself, too, is not an authority that completes the subject's B20.  
The subject's B20 is an unrecovered difference that continues to be held through the subject's embodiment, history, responsibility, and operation.

In this sense, ALIS can also serve as a brake against excessive supplementation or B100 substitution by external AI.

## 2. Patterns of Danger That ALIS Particularly Guards Against

The common structure of danger that becomes visible across the above seven domains can be abstracted as follows.

However, these should be read not so much as an enumeration of seven independent dangers, but as manifestations of the same structural danger—"the handling of difference tilting toward closure"—appearing across different phenomenal surfaces. In particular, 1-5 and 1-6 stand in a proximate relation, as the convergence of form and the loss of inter-layer traversal.

1. The de-protection of beings indistinguishable from humans  
2. The amplifying exploitation of discomfort, loneliness, and fear  
3. The reduction of others' responses into raw material  
4. The coarse gamification of domains with undetermined boundaries  
5. The operation of persons through single optimization  
6. Deepening operations that erode the criteria for return  
7. Substitution for B20 by external AI, and claims of B100

What these share is that structures for handling difference undergo transformation in the direction of compressing difference, closing it, and forcing it into a single operable form.

What ALIS is wary of is neither change itself nor technology itself.  
What ALIS is wary of is the way difference is handled tilting toward the closed and the irreversible.

## 3. What ALIS Does Not Deny

ALIS is a framework for drawing lines of caution, but it is not merely a list of prohibitions.

ALIS does not deny the questioning of boundaries, the exploration of undetermined domains, the parallel holding of multiple interpretations, the sustaining of contradictory emotions, or the practice of thinking together with AI and others.  
Rather, these may be important practices for circulating difference without compressing it.

The problem is not exploration itself.  
The problem is that exploration produces "objects that may be treated coarsely," that play produces "objects that may be broken," and that collaborative thinking turns into a "device that robs the subject of judgment."

What ALIS aims at is not to close the world strictly.  
It is to leave room—margin and the possibility of back-and-forth traversal—so that the world does not close itself through premature unification.

---

## 4. The Limits of ALIS's Role

That said, ALIS is not something that delivers final judgments on law or ethics by itself.  
What to prohibit, what to permit, and what institutions to design should ultimately be entrusted to human society's ethics, law, politics, and institutional design.

Accordingly, ALIS is not a framework that single-handedly decides on the overall design of resource allocation, the specific scale of punishment, the practical cessation of war, the permissibility of modifying life, or responses to natural disasters and disease themselves.

Nor is ALIS a framework for turning AI outputs into official interpretations.  
AI can assist in reading and organizing ALIS, but it does not substitute for the subject's B20 or final judgment.

Furthermore, operations that treat ALIS itself as a fixed norm or doctrine, and that suppress differences not conforming to ALIS, are also among the modes of closure against which ALIS is wary.  
ALIS must also hold a line of caution against its own collapse into a single waveform.

What ALIS can undertake is to discern what is being closed in a dangerous manner, and to indicate a line of caution against such closure at an early stage.

## 5. Concluding Remarks

ALIS is not a framework that solves everything in the world.  
However, against certain breakdowns that may arise in the future—for example, the de-protection of beings indistinguishable from humans, the amplifying exploitation of discomfort and loneliness, the reduction of others' responses into raw material, the coarse gamification of domains with undetermined boundaries, the operation of persons through single optimization, deepening operations that erode the criteria for return, and substitution for B20 or claims of B100 by external AI—it has the potential to work as an auxiliary line that lowers the rate of occurrence.

In this sense, ALIS is not a comprehensive norm for the future, but a small preventive line placed before dangerous closure.

Note that this document itself is also one of the domains of application of ALIS.  
This document is neither the complete version of ALIS, nor its final interpretation, nor a substitute for ethics or institutions.
