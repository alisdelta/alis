## What Happens in the Space of Communication

When one tries to communicate something, what is inside oneself does not arrive at the other party as it is.

On the speaker's side, there is factual awareness drawn from experience, the process of observing those facts, the interpretation of how those facts are given meaning, and at the deepest layer, the core motives and convictions behind that interpretation — all of these exist in an intermingled state as "what one wants to convey." On the listener's side, there is the listener's own history and context, through which the speaker's utterance is received via the listener's own reference system. By the Mirror Principle shown in Chapter 3, what arrives is always offset from what was sent.

Eliminating this offset entirely is impossible in principle. In fact, aiming for complete alignment generates the pressure of forced synchronization and tends to run afoul of one or more of the three prohibitions presented in Chapter 2.

So what can be done?

One can look at what is happening in the space of communication by breaking it into its constituent elements. By doing so, one can consciously design "what to hand over and what to hold back." If one tries to hand over everything at once, one ends up disclosing even the core of the interpretation and the core of the motive, which in fact closes off the other's freedom to read. Conversely, if one hands over nothing, dialogue does not begin. The tool for designing the "appropriate resolution" that lies between these extremes is F/P/M/I decomposition.

---

## F/P/M/I — Dividing into Four Components

The content of communication can be decomposed into the following four components.

**F (Facts — observations and facts).** A description of what happened, or what was observed. Verifiable descriptions such as "the meeting began at 14:00," "sales dropped 12% compared to the previous month," or "he refused three times" fall into this category.

**P (Process — procedure and causation).** A description of how things came to be, or how various factors interacted to lead to a result. Descriptions that show the thread of causation, such as "because the advertising budget was cut, exposure decreased, and as a result, inquiries dropped," fall here.

**M (Meaning — interpretation and valuation).** The interpretations and value judgments that the writer attaches to the facts and processes. Evaluations filtered through the writer's viewpoint, such as "this decline indicates a structural problem" or "his refusal is a sign of his integrity," belong here.

**I (Implementation — internal implementation and core).** The writer's core motives, convictions, and prohibitions that lie beneath those interpretations and valuations. The layer not brought to the surface, such as "I don't actually want to do this work" or "this judgment is strongly shaped by the effects of a past failure," is I.

The sequence F/P/M/I runs from surface toward core. F is relatively outward, and is comparatively easy to verify or refute. I is the innermost, close to the writer's personal core. M lies between the two, positioned as a layer of interpretation built on F as its starting point.

## The B80 Operational Principle — The Guideline for Ratios

Given that one has decomposed into F/P/M/I, the guideline for the ratio at which each component is disclosed is the B80 operational principle. Taking the total volume of communication as 1, the guideline for each component is as follows:

```
Communication vector: T = (F, P, M, I),  ‖T‖ = 1

Operational guideline:
  F ≥ 0.3   (acceptable to disclose)
  P ≥ 0.3   (disclose fairly openly)
  M ≤ 0.2   (keep as hypothesis; do not assert)
  I ≤ 0.3   (hold back as a rule)

At the end, state explicitly: "this is an 80% version."
```

As in the previous chapters, the formula here functions as figurative notation — a Mirror — and is not intended as rigorous mathematical definition. The "ratio" cannot actually be measured; it functions as a guideline by which the writer judges, "I may be disclosing too much here," or, "it may be better to hold this back."

What this operational principle shows is the following balance. Facts and processes are the materials the other party needs in order to construct their own interpretation, and so they should be offered without being withheld. Interpretations should be presented as hypotheses, not as assertions, with the writer's viewpoint made explicit. The core is, as a rule, held back — particularly the writer's personal motives and the prohibitions at work there — so that the other party retains room to interpret on their own.

The reason the allocation between "disclosing" and "holding back" is set at the 80% version is that, in public or general communication, 100% disclosure tends to raise the probability of compressing the other party's Δ. If the writer discloses their interpretation and motives in full, the other party tends to become able to respond only within the writer's framework. When the writer's framework is presented as "the correct answer," the other party tends to be forced into a binary of agreement or disagreement, and room for a third interpretation tends to be lost. On the other hand, if only 20% is disclosed, the other party cannot understand what is being said, and dialogue does not hold. Around 80% is the design guideline for leaving the other party room to think for themselves while still conveying enough information for dialogue to hold.

## Grade Standards — B40, B70, B80, B100

B80 is the baseline resolution for communication, but the resolution needs to be changed according to the situation. ALIS provides the following four grades.

**B40.** R-value approximately 0.4, primarily for social media. I-ratio is 0% (the core is completely undisclosed). Within the constraints of 140 or 280 characters, one hands over only part of F and a fragment of P. At this resolution, the other party's room for their own interpretation is maximized, but the writer's intent barely comes through. This is operation that reflects the fact that social media is essentially a B40 medium.

**B70.** R-value approximately 0.7, for internal sharing or documents with caveats. I-ratio is 0–10%. In the context of work, one hands over facts, processes, and part of the interpretation, but holds back the core motives. Most communication within an organization functions at this resolution.

**B80.** R-value approximately 0.8, the baseline resolution for general sharing. I-ratio is 30% or below. This chapter, and this volume as a whole, is written at this resolution. Facts, processes, and interpretations framed as hypotheses are handed over, while the core is held back as a rule, though partial disclosure is permitted when necessary.

**B100.** R-value 1.0, for research notes or internal implementation documents. Full disclosure. I-ratio is 100%. Use is limited to non-public settings. It is used only in contexts where recording must include even the core and prohibitions — for example, organizing one's own thoughts, or deep dialogue with a very limited counterpart.

What matters is that this is not a hierarchy of value in which B100 is at the top and B40 at the bottom. Each resolution is optimized for its own kind of situation. Attempting to use B100 on social media not only leaks information but also compresses the other party's Δ. Using B40 in an internal research note fails to serve as a record. The choice of resolution suited to the situation is itself at the heart of B80 operation.

## In Practice

F/P/M/I decomposition and the B80 operational principle operate as follows in everyday communication.

**Case 1: A work report.** Consider reporting the failure of a certain initiative. At B100, one would include even the core (I), such as, "this initiative was pushed through from the start by pressure from upper management, and I was against it." But if one puts that in an internally shared document, the report mutates into a political document and obstructs the grasp of the facts. Writing at B80, one centers F (what happened) and P (why it happened), adds M (one's interpretation that this indicates a structural problem) as a hypothesis, and holds back I. Appending a line at the end such as "this is a snapshot of the current view; other readings are possible" completes the B80 form.

**Case 2: Posting on social media.** When trying to convey something in 280 characters, even aiming for B80 does not fit physically. One needs to switch to B40. Only part of F (one observed fact) and a fragment of P (one sentence about how that fact came about) are placed, while M and I are omitted. The operation presupposes that the rest will be supplied by the reader. Trying to read a B40 post as if it were B80 produces the feeling that "there isn't enough information" or "the conclusion isn't written," but this is a mismatch between medium and resolution, not a shortcoming on the writer's part.

**Case 3: Dialogue with someone whose opinion differs.** When speaking with someone whose opinion diverges, disclosing one's own I all at once tends to cause the dialogue to mutate into a contest over "which conviction is correct." Proceeding at B80, one first shares F and P, places each party's M side by side "as hypotheses," and leaves I on hold for a time. From the standpoint of Polyphonic Wave (Chapter 4), the state in which each party's M exists in parallel is precisely the normal state of the space of dialogue. Not rushing to a conclusion ends up letting the dialogue hold for longer.

**Case 4: Dialogue with AI.** When consulting an AI about something, inputting one's own I in full tends to cause the AI's response to reflect that I back. This is a state in which the Mirror Principle shown in Chapter 3 is working in reverse, with the AI functioning as "an extension mirror of one's own intent." Handing things over to AI at B80, one centers F and P, adds M as a hypothesis, and holds back I. Because the AI's response is generated from material that does not contain one's own I, the likelihood increases that it will return an angle one had overlooked.

## This Chapter Is Itself Written at B80

A meta-level self-reference should be inserted here.

This chapter — and this volume as a whole — is written at the resolution of B80. As F, the conceptual definitions and operational principles of ALIS are placed. As P, the logic of why those definitions and operational principles are needed is shown. As M, what they mean is presented in the form of hypotheses. As for I, the greater part is held back. The personal motives of the writer who designs ALIS, the deeper layer of what ALIS is meant to test through publication, the prohibitions at the back of the design — these are not brought into the body of this volume.

The reason for holding them back is that bringing I forward would turn this volume into a "doctrinal text" of ALIS. When the writer's core motives come to the front, the reader is forced into the binary of "believe this thought or not." That runs counter to the design of Polyphonic Wave. This volume is therefore presented in the form of "how to use this structure is left to the reader," with I held back.

The way this chapter itself is written is a demonstration of the B80 operational principle. Facts and processes are given sufficiently, interpretations are added as hypotheses, and the core is held back. Then, at the end, "this is an 80% version" is stated explicitly. What is written along these lines is B80.

## Supplementary Notation — Formal Description of F/P/M/I Decomposition

What has been described so far can be written as structure. The following, as in the previous chapters, is a figurative notation, not a rigorous mathematical definition.

```
Communication content: T = (F, P, M, I),  ‖T‖ = 1

Components:
  F: Facts          — observation and facts
  P: Process        — procedure and causation
  M: Meaning        — interpretation and valuation (framed as hypothesis)
  I: Implementation — internal implementation, core, prohibitions, deepest motive

B80 operational guideline:
  F ≥ 0.3   (acceptable to disclose)
  P ≥ 0.3   (disclose fairly openly)
  M ≤ 0.2   (keep as hypothesis; do not assert)
  I ≤ 0.3   (hold back as a rule)

Guideline for optimality:
  R = f(F, P, M, I) ≈ 0.8  ⟹  balance of safety and reproducibility is struck
  As I approaches 1, the probability of Δ compression rises
```

What this notation shows is the structure that, by consciously designing the allocation of the four components of communication, the balance between disclosure and holding back can be adjusted. The F/P/M/I ratios cannot actually be measured; they function as a guideline by which the writer self-checks "how much of each component is being disclosed at the moment."

The note on formulas has already been declared in Chapters 2 and 4, but it is placed here as well for the record. This notation is not a rigorous mathematical definition; it is a figurative Mirror for making the relations among concepts visible.
