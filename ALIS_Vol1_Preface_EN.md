# Preface: What This Text Is and What It Is Not

---

What is about to be read is the first volume of an attempt called ALIS.

Before entering the main body, a few notes on how to read are placed here. This is not a summary of the contents. It is a chapter whose role is only to prepare, a little, the stance of the reader, before the structural discussions from Chapter 1 onward begin.

What this preface touches on is the minimal line-drawing of what ALIS is not. A description of what ALIS is will be left to Chapter 1 and beyond.

---

## Not a Doctrine

ALIS is not a doctrine.

It does not ask to be believed in, nor does it presuppose agreement. It does not offer any salvation or benefit to be gained by accepting something as correct.

Within this book, proprietary vocabulary, formulas, and structural descriptions appear. On the surface, these may resemble some system of thought or system of belief. However, what ALIS addresses is only the structural relations in the fields of perception, dialogue, and transmission. It does not point to any object to be believed in.

Readers may read ALIS while doubting it. Doubting and reading hold together in the operation of ALIS.

---

## Not a Completed Answer

ALIS is not a presentation of completed answers.

How to live in the age of AI, the correct way to handle human relationships, the direction society should head toward — ALIS holds no definitive answers to such questions. What ALIS presents is one ordering concerning how to position oneself in specific scenes, what to preserve, and what to avoid.

An ordering is lighter than an answer. An answer aims at a state that is no longer to be questioned, whereas an ordering leaves room for the next question to arise. What ALIS aims at is the latter.

Therefore, even after reading this book to the end, nothing is completely resolved. Differences, uncertainties, and undecided matters remain after reading as well. This does not mean the reader's reading was insufficient. It is only that ALIS is designed that way.

---

## Not a Defense of Ambiguity

Because ALIS adopts postures such as "preserving difference," "holding contradictions at once," and "not converging on a single answer," it may be read as "a defense of ambiguity" or "a philosophy of anything goes."

It is worth denying this even at the level of the preface.

ALIS does not defend ambiguity in general. What can be made clear is made clear. In scenes where a decision must be made, a decision is made. However, the movements of forcing clarity onto what cannot be made clear, or hastily deciding what need not be decided, are treated as structural problems. The design of that distinction is the content of ALIS.

The detailed distinctions are developed from Chapter 2 onward. Here, it is noted in a single line in advance that ALIS does not take the position of "anything goes."

---

## Not a Direct Prescription

ALIS is not a prescription for the salvation of society.

To the various problems of the age of AI — the excess of information, division, synchronization pressure, the short-circuiting of judgment — ALIS cannot hold any direct solution. What ALIS provides is an auxiliary line concerning how the human who stands in the field of such problems maintains their own stance.

An auxiliary line only shows what becomes visible in the field of the one who draws it; it holds no power to move the problems of society as a whole. It is a tool to be used in individual fields, by individual people, for their own judgments.

This limitation is not modesty; it is an explicit statement of scope. Trying to make it work broadly lowers the precision in individual fields. Prioritizing precision in individual fields is the character of ALIS's design.

---

## Scenes Where It Can Be Used

Scenes where ALIS may function as an auxiliary line include, for example, the following.

- Moments of hesitation, in dialogue with AI, about how much of the response to take on as one's own
- When trying to post briefly on social media, feeling that something is being lost between what one wants to convey and the constraints of the format
- When, in a conversation with someone of differing opinion, one wishes to proceed without settling which side is correct
- When, in the decision-making fields of an organization, the positions of dissenting views buried beneath the appearance of consensus begin to stand out

In these scenes, ALIS does not necessarily prove useful. If, after drawing it, nothing becomes visible, one may close it as it is. This book is written on the premise of such use.

---

## On How to Read

ALIS is written to be read in order, but does not require a strict sequence.

Chapter 2 (Δ and the Three Prohibitions) and Chapter 5 (The Resolution of Communication) are closer to practice than the other chapters. Readers who want practical tools before conceptual discussion may start with these. In that case, the remaining chapters can be referred to afterward as needed.

It is also not necessary to read every chapter. At the end of Chapter 5, it is noted once that practice is possible even without a second volume. In the final chapter, it is noted that this first volume closes, once. Whether to read further, how many times to reread, whether to cite and use, whether it is fine to forget — these belong to the reader's own domain.

---

The above are the minimal notes before entering the main body.

What ALIS is is written from Chapter 1 onward. The remaining parts of what it is not — the difference from relativism, the difference from anti-AI arguments, the difference from religion — will be taken up in Chapter 7, after Chapters 1 through 6 have been read.

The preface closes here.
