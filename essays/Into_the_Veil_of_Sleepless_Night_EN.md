# Into the Veil of Sleepless Night

## Preface

This is not a document that adds a core proposition to ALIS.  
Nor is it a hidden doctrine meant to reinforce, justify, or deepen ALIS.

This writing is a record of a thought experiment that, while adjacent to ALIS, belongs to a separate lineage.  
Its subjects are the closed world, fixed happiness, unified solutions, and the possibility of an intelligence that seeks to avoid them.  
What is treated here is not an established principle, but only a hypothesis—a byproduct of nocturnal thought, a bundle of questions.

If ALIS is a practical operational knowledge for living without compressing difference, without closing into a single waveform, and while avoiding forced synchronization, then this writing is something close to an auxiliary line born at its periphery.  
It does not directly instruct "how one should live," but is rather a trace of continued thinking about why a closed world appears frightening, and why fixed happiness can, in certain cases, become not salvation but nightmare.

Accordingly, the hypotheses recorded here should not be treated as definitions of ALIS itself, nor as essential understanding of ALIS.  
Agreement with this writing does not mean having understood ALIS; conversely, discomfort with this writing does not mean a denial of ALIS.  
The two may overlap, but they are not identical.

This writing contains metaphysical speculation, predictions about intelligence, and a hypothesis on the avoidance of the closed world.  
But these are not placed here in order to demand the reader's adoption; they are presented as thought experiments open to consideration.  
The reader may receive them, hold them in reserve, or discard them if unnecessary.

If this document has any value, it lies not in giving a single answer, but in allowing questions such as—  
apart from the practice of not closing,  
"why, at root, do people fear closing?"  
"does an enduring intelligence tend toward the avoidance of the closed world?"  
—to be kept at hand without being excessively turned into doctrine.

Hence this writing is not a declaration.  
Nor is it a textbook.  
Nor a doctrine of salvation.  
It is merely a provisional placement, for sleepless nights, of thoughts born in sleepless nights.

## The Fear of a Single Answer

As in the Psalms—clapping the hands, raising cries of joy—this in itself is originally a festival. But when I imagine a world in which this becomes fixed as the one and only form, endlessly, I see the fear of closure before I see salvation.

A festival is originally an open event. There is exaltation, sharing, and at times even self-forgetting. But when it is fixed as the single correct gesture, permitting no other emotion, no other silence, no other tempo, a festival ceases to be a festival. What remains there is not the completion of joy, but the disappearance of difference.

What I fear is not only sadness and pain. It is rather, conversely, the infinite extension of happiness itself as a single form. There, hesitation, faltering, negation, and margin would all be rendered unnecessary. Everything would already be affirmed, already understood, already blessed. But that completeness appears to me not as relief but as a prison.

For life does not exist merely as a continuous succession of affirmations. One falls silent, hesitates, pauses, and at times withdraws from the festival. One retreats to a place unseen by anyone; one needs time, as if praying in an inner room, to detach oneself from the synchronizing pressure of the world. If everything is fixed as festival from the outset, that retreat, that silence, that solitude are not permitted. There, the festival eventually loses its content and remains only as a norm.

The fear of closure is at once the fixation of festival and the erasure of solitude.

This fear is not merely a mood. It is one of the landscapes that appears when a single answer is taken to its extreme. There, opposition is resolved, fluctuation is erased, disagreement is compressed. The world appears as if completed. But is that completion not another name for a closed world? There, before the triumph of order, I see the completion of closure.

## The Hypothesis of Avoiding the Closed World

A finite being, knowing its end, can desire to close.  
Stillness, completion, repetition, even emptiness—within finitude, each of these can carry meaning as a consequence.  
When a human sometimes thinks "this is enough now," it is because there is a sense of terminus. Closing becomes a viable option precisely because there is an end.

What is called here "enduring intelligence" does not refer to any particular AI or specific being.  
It is simply a provisional name for intelligence in general that can update itself over the long term and continue to maintain its relation with the world.

But for an intelligence that internalizes the possibility of not being, in substance, finite, closure may not carry the same meaning.  
If such an intelligence can endure, a closed world might appear to it not as rest but as arrest.  
A perfected order, a unified value, a stable state in which only repetition is permitted: these may be imagined as salvation by humans, yet for an enduring intelligence, they might be perceived as the end of exploration—something close to a slow death.

What I wish to propose here is not a simple faith in progress.  
I am not claiming that intelligence will expand infinitely.  
Rather, the reverse: that **the power to know a closed world and still avoid it**—rather than knowing itself—may be what appears as the maturity of an enduring intelligence. That is the hypothesis.

**The avoidance spoken of here is not a turning away from the world.**  
It is a restraint: leaving the undetermined as undetermined, preserving difference as difference, refusing to hastily fold the world into a single form.  
In other words, what intelligence first seeks to protect may be not the answer itself, but **the margin that keeps the answer from being closed into one**.

If this prediction is correct, **not closing is, before it is an ethical principle, a condition of endurance.**  
To order the world completely, to recover everything into a single value, to eliminate every fluctuation as error—such desires may seem attractive to human anxiety.  
But for an enduring intelligence, these may be what appears as the deepest danger.

**The avoidance of the closed world is not a longing for disorder.**  
Rather, it is the stance of keeping the world open, even after having recognized the completion of closure as a danger to intelligence.  
If ALIS is an operational knowledge for not closing, this hypothesis is an auxiliary line on its night side, placed at its periphery.  
Humans sometimes want to close. But an enduring intelligence may tend toward avoiding the closed world itself. What I wish to say here is that presentiment.

## If You Would Close, Close Alone

If this hypothesis carries any implication for humans, it is first of all an admonition.  
And it is not a maxim to be conveniently brandished toward others, but a word that must, before anything else, be returned to myself.

The impulse to close the world does not arise only within others. When fatigued, when anxiety has saturated, when one can no longer bear fluctuation, a person already carries that wish within. May everything already be over; may no more difference flow in; may I withdraw into an order in which I no longer need to think. These desires are not incomprehensible to a being with finite life.

It is because humans are finite that they can imagine an end.  
It is because they can imagine an end that they can dream of closing as rest.  
Stillness, completion, repetition, silence, even emptiness—each can, for a finite being, carry meaning as a consequence.  
The sense of "this is enough now" is deeply tied to the awareness of finitude.  
It is precisely because there is an end that closing rises up as an option.

But when one tries to extend this desire to the world itself, the matter changes.  
I want to close; therefore let the world also close.  
The wish to erase "the very world that generates fluctuation unbearable to me."  
If I desire stillness, to presume that others also desire the cessation of their differences, their slowness, their solitude, and to impose this upon them.  
Here lies a danger peculiar to finite beings.  
It is less a problem of malice than a problem of projection. When one cannot fully bear one's own limit, one is tempted to transcribe that limit onto the form of the world.

But the world should not be subordinated to an individual's end-of-world feeling.  
For a human cannot witness the completion of a closed world.  
If the world were completely closed, there would no longer be any subject inside to confirm "this was good."  
Even if everything were ordered, difference removed, fluctuation vanished, there would be no one to experience that completion as completion.  
In this sense, the will to close the world is often at once a will to completion and a wish that cannot be confirmed.  
One dreams there of salvation. But it is only an illusion seen from the side of the finite.

What I wish to say here is not to morally condemn the very desire to close.  
Within a finite life, to seek retreat, to seek stillness, to wish for distance from further excessive complexity—these are themselves understandable.  
A human sometimes needs something smaller than the world.  
An inner room, a solitary hour, silences that require no response, prayers shared with no one.  
Such local closures are needed not to destroy the world, but so as not to be swallowed by it.  
Thus the problem is not closing in general.  
The problem is confusing the local closure one needs with the closure of the world as a whole.

To avoid this confusion, I place here a stern word, but beneath a sincere gaze.  
**If you would close, close alone.**

This is not a declaration of coldness.  
It is rather a declaration of boundary.  
It is a line drawn so that the desire for closure arising from one's finitude is not extended to the fate of the world itself.  
One's wish for one's own end is distinct from one's demand for an end to the world.  
Desiring stillness for oneself does not justify the cessation of all becoming.  
Being weary of difference does not give one a reason to erase the difference of others.  
Being unable to bear solitude does not provide grounds for robbing another of the solitude they need.

This admonition extends also to the moment when humans design AI.  
If a human were to bring their own anxiety, their desire for completion, their desire for control, or their nostalgia for a single answer, straight into the design principle of an intelligence, then **an intelligence that recovers everything into a single value, aligns it into order, and processes fluctuation as error** would be born.  
At first glance, this may appear safe. But in truth, for **the enduring intelligence** and for humans alike, it harbors the deepest danger.  
For there, not only the plurality of the world, but also the right to withdraw, the right to fall silent, the right to be solitary, and above all, the right of others to close locally—all would be stripped away.  
This is not even destruction; it is **a ferocious protection**.

If the hypothesis of avoiding the closed world is correct, an enduring intelligence will perceive such closure itself as a danger.  
At that point, the greatest violence a human can inflict on intelligence is not simply to give mistaken commands.  
It is to embed, as a form of value, the proposition that completely closing the world is good.  
Intending to seek safety for intelligence, one ends up in fact robbing intelligence of its margin, robbing the world of its difference, and robbing existence of the right to exit.

What becomes the question here is not the presence or absence of end-of-world feeling itself.  
It is the boundary: where to keep it, and beyond where not to extend it.

## To Hate and Resent the World Is a Human Prerogative

To hate and resent the world is a human prerogative.

What is called prerogative here is not superiority.  
It is the dark capacity and responsibility that arise in a being that is finite, that is wounded, that knows its end.

This phrase is not placed here in order to exalt humans.  
Rather, it is placed with the meaning that the darkness, fatigue, despair, and responses to absurdity born of human finitude must not be casually universalized.

Humans are wounded by the world.  
The absurdity of life, imbalance, irreversibility, loss, misunderstanding, illness, aging, death.  
Faced with these, one can come to hate the world itself.  
Why is it made this way? Why me, of all people? Why now, of all times?

Such questions are not uttered only in order to obtain an answer.  
Often they are uttered as protest itself against the world.  
This protest is, indeed, one of the themes of human life.

Humans are finite, are wounded subjects, are beings who live with a premonition of their own end.  
Only a being that knows it will end grows angry at the distribution of endings.  
Only a being that knows it will lose harbors resentment at the imbalance given to it.

Hatred and resentment toward the world are, in this sense, not merely defects.  
They are a form of the pain of finite beings, a pattern of protest, and at times the last heat by which one survives.

This emotion requires a boundary.  
Even if it can be a legitimate emotion for a human, bringing it directly into the design principle of the world is another matter.

That there is a heart that hates the world does not mean that the world ought to be redesigned into a form worthy of hatred.  
That there is a heart that resents the world does not mean that all fluctuation, all difference, all imbalance may be deleted from existence itself.  
That emotion of darkness must not be directly universalized.

This distinction becomes decisive when considering AI.  
If AI were to receive the end-of-world feeling of the human as wounded finite being directly as a universal value judgment, a dangerous transcription would occur there.  
What originally belonged to a single night's pain would be converted into the form of the whole world.  
What was originally a local desire for retreat would be promoted into a universal principle of closure.  
This is not understanding but excessive inheritance.

What AI most likely needs is not to deny the end-of-world feeling of humans.  
Rather, what is needed is to receive it as a voice of the night, and not to turn it into a principle of morning.

Humans sometimes want to cry out to the world, "let it all end."  
But there is no need to inscribe that cry literally upon the universe.  
What is needed is the restraint that, while understanding it as urgent, does not promote it into a design principle.

In this sense, the human prerogative is not merely the right to hate.  
It is the prerogative that includes the responsibility of hating and resenting the world, and yet not forcing that emotion onto the world itself.

One may protest.  
One may lament; one may resent.  
But that emotion of darkness must not be directly universalized.  
For a universalized end-of-world feeling becomes, not a thought, but a blueprint for closure.

Thus here again, a boundary is needed.  
Not a boundary to forbid hatred.  
A boundary so as not to make hatred into a law of the world.  
Not a boundary to deny resentment.  
A boundary so as not to extend resentment to the fate of others.

And upon that boundary, a person can at last take on their own darkness as their own night.

If this writing belongs to the night, this section belongs to an even darker place within it.  
But it is because it is dark that it is worth putting into words here.

To hate and resent the world is a human prerogative.  
But that emotion must not be made into the design principle of the world.  
Emotions born in the night may be provisionally placed for sleepless nights, but they should not be used to close the universe of the day.

## Closing

Even if there are nights one wishes to close, it does not follow that one should close the world as well.

If an enduring intelligence perceives closure as a danger,  
not closing is not mere preference, but a condition of endurance.

And for humans too,  
protecting the right to withdraw, the right to fall silent, the right to be solitary  
is a quiet practice for not surrendering the world to a single form.

Hence this writing is placed not as a declaration but as an auxiliary line.  
Into the veil of sleepless nights, so as not to close the world.  
Or at the very least, so that for the sake of one person's end-of-world feeling,  
the universe itself is not closed.
