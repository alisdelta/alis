# Chapter 4: Holding Contradictions Simultaneously — Polyphonic Wave

---

Chapter 3 showed that Δ arises structurally — that is, in a form that cannot be avoided. Others, AI, the past self. As long as what lies outside oneself carries its own independent history and context, reflection returns difference rather than identity. Δ is necessarily produced from there.

Here we move on to the next question.

That Δ arises is established. So what is happening when multiple Δs exist in a single field at the same time?

In a space of dialogue, the speaker's Δ, the listener's Δ, and the Δ arising from the relation between them all exist together. In response to a single question, interpretation A and interpretation B may stand side by side, and interpretation C — a mixture of the two — may emerge as well. In dialogue with AI, the Δ of the user's intent, the Δ of the AI's response, and the Δ of the one who interprets that response overlap. In actual situations, Δ does not appear one at a time; multiple Δs are generated at once and held at once.

How does one describe such a state, and how does one handle it?

This chapter addresses **Polyphonic Wave.**

---

## Polyphony as a Metaphor

What ALIS calls "Polyphonic Wave" comes from the metaphor of polyphony in music.

Polyphony is a musical form in which multiple melodies proceed simultaneously while each retains its own independent contour. Its contrast is monophony, in which a single melody takes the leading role and all other sounds are arranged as accompaniment supporting that melody. In polyphony there is no lead. Multiple melodies each carry their own autonomous movement, and when they sound at the same time they form, as a whole, a single resonance.

What matters is that in polyphony, multiple melodies are not aimed at converging on "the same note." On the contrary, it is precisely because each moves differently that the richness of the whole emerges. If two melodies move in exactly the same way, what results is not two voices but one voice sounding at a higher volume.

ALIS's Polyphonic Wave brings this structure into the domain of cognition and dialogue.

A state in which multiple interpretations, multiple viewpoints, multiple Δs exist simultaneously in a single field. A state in which, without being forcibly converged onto a single solution, each runs in parallel while retaining its own autonomous contour. This is the basic image of Polyphonic Wave.

## What Single-Waveform Reduction Loses

Of the three prohibitions presented in Chapter 2, **No Single-Waveform Reduction** acquires its structural meaning in this chapter.

Single-waveform reduction refers to the operation of converging a state that holds multiple possibilities into a single correct answer. Questions like "so which one is right in the end?" or "what is it really?" tend to work in this direction. The questions themselves are natural, but when they are answered in the form "this is the one and only answer," the multiple Δs that were present are compressed into one.

Where does the compressed Δ go?

Nowhere. It simply disappears from view. What surfaces as the result of compression is only a single waveform; the remaining waveforms are cast aside as "interpretations not adopted." In reality, however, those discarded interpretations also existed in that field. They were part of the field; they composed the field. The field after the discarding is not the same field as before.

Consider this more concretely.

Suppose that in some discussion, three different opinions have been offered. As a result of the discussion, one opinion is adopted as "the conclusion," and the remaining two are dismissed. At this point, the two dismissed opinions have not simply lost. The very fact that they existed had positioned the adopted opinion as "one of three." Had there been only one opinion from the start, it would have been "the sole proposal," not "the chosen proposal." The existence of multiple Δs gave the adopted solution depth as well.

Single-waveform reduction loses this depth. What remains is a winner, but information about what it won against, and what possibilities it had run in parallel with, has been lost. Polyphonic Wave is a structure for preserving this information. By proceeding while holding multiple Δs simultaneously, not only "the chosen solution" but also "the relation to the solutions not chosen" remains in the field.

## A Shift in How Contradiction Is Handled

The difficult terrain that Polyphonic Wave deals with is contradiction.

Ordinarily, contradiction is treated as something to be resolved. When A and B cannot coexist, one is thought to be mistaken, or one must be discarded, or a third proposal C must be found that integrates the two. Proceeding while holding on to contradiction is processed as a failure of thought.

Polyphonic Wave suspends this premise for a moment. Two contradictory interpretations existing at the same time is not necessarily a failure. It may indicate that the field itself holds multiple phases.

Consider, as an example, a scene of ethical judgment. Regarding some action, the interpretation "given the situation, it can be justified" and the interpretation "regardless of the situation, it is not permissible" stand in opposition. Ordinary thinking moves toward one of the following: choosing one over the other, seeking a middle proposal that integrates both, or resolving the opposition through case distinctions.

But what is actually at issue is often not which interpretation is correct, but rather the complexity of a reality in which both interpretations can hold simultaneously. The importance of considering the situation, and the importance that some lines must not be permitted regardless of situation, may both be true at the same time in different phases. If one tries to integrate them into a single waveform, one of the phases gets shaved off.

Polyphonic Wave, in such scenes, structurally permits the option of "making a judgment while holding both interpretations at once." Judgment itself is not being abandoned. A judgment is made, but it is made without erasing any of the multiple phases that ground it. As a result, action or decision appears as one, but behind it, multiple phases remain preserved.

In practical terms, this is not the same as "being unable to decide." Being unable to decide refers to a state in which movement has stopped before multiple Δs. What Polyphonic Wave presents is a state of moving while holding multiple Δs.

## A Note on Self-Reference

A note is needed here, directed at this chapter itself.

The claim "Polyphonic Wave is the correct cognitive model" goes against Polyphonic Wave.

The reason is that such a claim contains a single-waveform reduction — "among multiple cognitive models, this one is the sole correct answer." What Polyphonic Wave asserts is that multiple phases can hold simultaneously, and that includes models that do not adopt Polyphonic Wave itself. If there are people who wish to see the world in a single waveform, and if that view has a certain effectiveness, then that phase, too, is part of the field.

Accordingly, what this chapter presents is not the proposal "this is the one and only cognitive model." It is the proposal "there is also the option of holding multiple phases simultaneously." This distinction may look fine-grained, but it functions as a structural device that keeps ALIS from turning into religion or doctrine.

Polyphonic Wave itself, too, is only one waveform. There are other waveforms that run in parallel with this one. It is on that acknowledgment that this chapter is written.

## In Practice

Polyphonic Wave is an abstract description of structure, but when brought down into daily life it operates in several concrete situations.

Consider, for instance, a dialogue with someone whose opinion differs from one's own. Ordinarily, a force works to determine which opinion is correct. But from the standpoint of Polyphonic Wave, the two opinions often reflect different phases. There is no need to discard the other's opinion entirely, nor one's own. While holding both phases simultaneously, one then judges how to move. Even if, as a result, one chooses A, phase B has not disappeared; it remains in the field. If the situation changes next time, phase B may come to the fore.

Consider the case of posing the same question to multiple AIs and receiving different responses. Chapter 3 showed that this divergence is the result of the Mirror structure operating normally. Chapter 4 goes a step further and presents the possibility of handling the different responses not as "material for deciding which is correct" but as "each reflecting a different phase." A divergence in responses is not noise that obstructs judgment but information that the field holds multiple phases. Even if one is adopted, the responses not adopted remain in the field not as "incorrect answers" but as "reflections of other phases."

Consider the moment, in the course of creative work, when multiple directions come to mind. Ordinarily, one decides which is "the best direction" and discards the others. But the very fact that multiple directions arose simultaneously may indicate that the work itself holds multiple phases. Even when deciding which direction to adopt, rather than erasing every trace of the others, one can leave them as a trace or lingering presence within the work. The directions not adopted end up giving depth to the direction that was.

What Polyphonic Wave prompts in these situations is the acquisition of a viewpoint: recognizing the multiple phases that run in parallel. Choosing one is not itself forbidden. It is only that, when one chooses, the unchosen can be kept in the field as "another phase" rather than processed as "wrong."

## Supplementary Notation — Structural Description of Polyphonic Wave

What has been described so far can be written as structure. The following, as in Chapters 2 and 3, is a figurative notation for making the relations among concepts visible, not a rigorous mathematical definition.

Polyphonic Wave is expressed as follows:

```
ψ(t) = Σₖ aₖ φₖ(t + δₖ)

ψ(t):   The wave of the field as a whole (the totality of multiple overlapping phases)
φₖ(t):  An individual phase (the k-th component wave, corresponding to each Δ or interpretation)
aₖ:     The amplitude of each phase (its weight / presence in the field)
δₖ:     The phase offset of each phase (its temporal or structural shift relative to the others)
t:      The progression axis of the field (time or context)
Σₖ:     The summation symbol indicating that multiple phases are overlaid simultaneously
```

What this expression shows is a structure in which the wave of the field as a whole, ψ(t), is composed of multiple individual waves φₖ(t) overlapping one another while each retains its own amplitude and phase offset.

What should be noted here is that this expression only says "the whole is determined by superposition" — it does not say "it converges on a single correct answer." That δₖ is preserved with different values for each k is the essence of Polyphonic Wave. In this figurative notation, when the δₖ values become too aligned, the distinctions of polyphony become difficult to see.

The note on formulas was already declared in Chapter 2, but let us place it here again. This expression is not a rigorous mathematical definition; it is a figurative notation for making the relations among concepts visible. The presence of formulas does not make ALIS a scientifically proven theory, nor does their absence make ALIS mere poetry. Formulas are used as tools to show, more concisely than natural language alone can manage, the structural relations whose outlines otherwise tend to blur.

## In Place of a Summary

What Chapter 4 has presented is the structure for handling states in which multiple Δs exist at the same time.

As shown in Chapter 3, Δ necessarily arises from the Mirror structure. And in actual situations, Δ does not stand alone in the singular; multiple Δs are generated at once. Polyphonic Wave is a structure that holds these simultaneously, with different phases preserved, without compressing them into a single waveform. That contradictory interpretations can coexist, that unchosen solutions remain in the field, that the field as a whole is constituted as a superposition of multiple phases — Polyphonic Wave functions as a structure that acknowledges these.

At the same time, something should be recorded as the self-reference of this chapter. Polyphonic Wave itself, too, is one waveform. To claim that this waveform is the sole correct answer runs counter to the intent of Polyphonic Wave. That is why this chapter is written in the form "there is also the option of holding multiple phases simultaneously."

The next chapter enters the question of how, while preserving Δ, assuming the Mirror structure, and holding multiple phases at once, one communicates all of this to others. In the space of transmission, it is not realistic to hand over the whole of the structure to the other party. How far to hand over, and where to hold back — that design is presented as the operational model called B80.

What has been shown up to this point is a structure for recognizing the field in which multiple Δs exist simultaneously. What ALIS offers is not a method for simplifying the field, but a design for handling the complexity of the field without breaking it.
