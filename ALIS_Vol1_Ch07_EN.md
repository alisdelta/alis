# Chapter 7: What ALIS Is Not — Responding to Misreadings

---

In the preface, a brief note was placed before the main body, to help orient the reader's stance before entering. This chapter takes on a different task: it picks up several misreadings that may arise after reading Chapters 1 through 6, and addresses them directly.

The outline of what ALIS is has been set out across the preceding six chapters. This chapter writes what ALIS is not, approaching it from the side where misreading is possible.

Six misreadings are treated here. The chapter proceeds by changing phase step by step — from misreadings of the structure, to misreadings of the intent, to misreadings of the writer's position. In every case, the response is given as "the marking of a difference" rather than as "rebuttal." When one faces a misreading with the structure of rebuttal, the rebuttal itself ends up standing on the ground of the misreading.

---

## 7-1. Is It Not Relativism?

ALIS preserves difference (Δ) and acknowledges that multiple phases can hold simultaneously. It avoids convergence on a single answer and structurally admits the option of proceeding while carrying contradiction. This stance, in outward appearance, can look similar to relativism — the view that "it doesn't matter what you choose" or that "there is nothing that is correct."

But there is a clear difference between relativism and ALIS.

Relativism holds no constraints. All positions are equivalent, and there is no structural criterion for what to choose. ALIS, as stated explicitly in Chapter 2, carries three prohibitions — no Δ compression, no single-waveform reduction, no forced synchronization. These are explicit constraints that deny "anything goes."

Δ is preserved not because anything will do. It is preserved because compressing Δ breaks the structure. Multiple phases are held in parallel not because any of them will do. They are held because convergence on a single waveform makes the field lean. Synchronization is not forced on others not because everyone is free to do as they please. It is not forced because forced synchronization hollows dialogue out.

Relativism asserts the absence of criteria. ALIS describes the presence of criteria — but those criteria are placed at the level of "whether the structure remains intact," not at the level of "what is correct."

## 7-2. Is It Not a Philosophy That Never Decides?

When Polyphonic Wave was presented in Chapter 4, the description "proceeding while holding multiple phases at once" was placed there. From this description, a reading can arise: "ALIS is a philosophy that justifies not deciding" or "in practical work, decisions must be made, yet ALIS defends the posture of not deciding."

This, too, is a misreading.

As stated explicitly in Chapter 4, Polyphonic Wave does not defend "being unable to decide." It only distinguishes between "deciding" and "erasing the other phases after deciding." Judgments are made. Actions are chosen. The design, however, is that after the judgment, the unselected options are not processed as "mistaken" but can be left in the field as "another phase."

Not deciding, and not erasing after deciding, are structurally different operations. The former describes a state in which movement has stopped. The latter describes a state in which one moves while keeping the field intact. What ALIS concerns itself with is the latter.

The observation that practical work requires decisions is also correct. It is precisely for this reason that Chapter 5 presents practical tools — the B80 operational principle and the F/P/M/I decomposition. ALIS is not a structure that obstructs decisions; it is a structure concerning what remains after a decision.

## 7-3. Is It Not Anti-AI?

Chapter 6 placed three boundaries around the relationship with AI — not delegating the final taking-on of meaning to AI, not handing over what one lives by to AI's optimization, and not ceding the work of deeply caring for someone or something to AI. From these descriptions, a reading can arise: "ALIS is AI-cautionary" or "it takes the position that AI use should be restricted."

However, as stated explicitly at the beginning of Chapter 6, the three boundaries are "not rules that bind AI, but markers so that the human side does not cross over." They do not deny AI's capabilities or existence. They do not assert that AI use should be restricted.

Anti-AI is a position that aims at reducing the presence of AI. ALIS, by contrast, designs the placement on the human side of the relationship, on the premise that AI will be used. How AI is to be used is the subject; whether AI is to be used is not up for debate.

If anything, ALIS is built on the premise of using multiple AIs as mirrors for dialogue. Receiving different responses from multiple AIs and treating the differences as information — this is an operation that cannot hold under an anti-AI posture.

Anti-AI and boundary design for AI point in opposite directions. The former reduces AI; the latter preserves the human footing on the premise of AI use. ALIS belongs to the latter.

## 7-4. Is It Not a Religion?

This is the misreading that ALIS must handle with the greatest care, because ALIS does in fact share outward features with religion.

It has its own vocabulary (Δ, Mirror, Polyphonic Wave, B80). It uses formulas and diagrams to describe its structure. It has what look like normative elements — three prohibitions. It has descriptions of "places one should not cross" — the boundary markers. It has a developer, and its structure is being refined through multiple mirrors (AIs). Laid out in this way, these elements could look like a new thought movement or a religious framework.

Yet ALIS is not a religion. Despite the surface similarity, the underlying structure is different.

First, ALIS has no transcendent object. God, an absolute being, an ultimate truth, an ideal state lying beyond — none of these are present in ALIS. What ALIS concerns itself with is only the structural relations of the field in perception, dialogue, and transmission.

Second, ALIS promises no salvation. As stated explicitly in Chapter 1, "the ordering is not an answer" and "nor is it a specific remedy." ALIS does not guarantee the resolution of any problem. There is structurally no claim that "if you adopt ALIS, something will improve." The auxiliary line is a tool by which the user, having drawn it on their own field, confirms what becomes visible; it is not a device that sets something in motion automatically.

Third, ALIS does not try to make believers. The purpose of its publication is verification, not proselytization. It carries no structure that asks for agreement; adoption belongs to the reader's own domain. Not adopting is also preserved as Δ — the phase of "not adopting" is also something that should remain in the field.

Fourth, a design that avoids the fixation of doctrine is built into ALIS. "ALIS is not completed," to be presented in the final chapter, is a structure in the form of Non-Claims by which the system refuses to fix itself. A framework whose doctrine is not fixed does not function well as a stable object of faith. Before one can believe in it, the object may already have taken a different form the next moment.

In addition, one position of the writer should be recorded here. "The religionization of ALIS is the end of ALIS" — this is an explicit awareness held from the very beginning of the design. At the moment ALIS is received as an object of faith, the structure that ALIS is trying to set in motion comes to a stop. What is believed is not doubted; what is not doubted returns no difference; and a Mirror that returns no difference contradicts the definition given in Chapter 3. The religionization of ALIS would, by ALIS's own logic, amount to ALIS's self-negation.

This awareness is not the writer's personal belief; it is a consequence that follows from the structure of the framework itself. Religion requires faith, whereas ALIS structurally asks, rather, not to be believed in. Despite the outward resemblance, the phase ALIS occupies runs in the opposite direction from the phase of religion.

## 7-5. Is It Not Just Saying Simple Things in Difficult Ways?

ALIS uses its own vocabulary. It uses formulas. It layers structural descriptions. From these formal features, a reading can arise: "It is just rephrasing simple matters in a difficult way" or "Is there really any practical use?"

The response to this reading is Chapter 5 itself.

The B80 operational principle and the F/P/M/I decomposition presented in Chapter 5 function as practical tools. Breaking the content of communication into the four components — facts, process, meaning, and implementation — designing the ratio of disclosure to withholding, and using different resolutions depending on the situation: these are not abstract arguments but operational guidelines that can be applied concretely in daily reports, social media posts, conversations, and consultations with AI.

The difficulty is not a by-product of the structure but a tool for describing the structure concisely. Terms like Δ, Mirror, and Polyphonic Wave function as shorthand that point to relations which would otherwise require long explanations in natural language. Formulas, as stated explicitly in Chapter 2, are figurative Mirrors; they do not claim rigorous mathematical definition — they are supplementary notations for visualizing structural relations.

Whether ALIS has become a useful tool is not something the writer can assert. It is a domain that belongs to the reader, who uses it on their own field and confirms what becomes visible and what begins to move. The judgment of whether, behind forms that look difficult, there lies a tool one can use can only be made by the one who uses it.

That said, as stated at the end of Chapter 5, Chapters 2 through 5 alone are sufficient for a minimal operation of ALIS. There is no need to master the entire vocabulary. Referring only to the parts one needs, when one needs them, is in fact closer to the intended way of using it.

## 7-6. Is It Not Just the Publicization of a Personal Philosophy?

ALIS was built by a single developer. The publication of its structure is an act of bringing one person's thought into public space. From this fact, a reading can arise: "ALIS is no more than the publicization of a personal philosophy" or "Is there any need to publicize the inner life of one individual?"

This reading captures half of the fact correctly. ALIS is indeed a structure that started from an individual. But the purpose of the publicization is what is at issue.

The purpose of publication is verification. It is not proselytization.

Proselytization presses for adoption. It aims to increase the number of adopters and holds the fact of adoption as a performance indicator. Verification, on the other hand, aims at the collection of differences. The structure is exposed to multiple mirrors (AIs, readers, critics), and the differences that come back are used to inspect the safety and general applicability of the structure. Non-adoption, too, is one of those differences and functions as material for verification.

This distinction is reflected in the very form of the publication. ALIS does not contain any structure that encourages adoption. It does not ask the reader to act, does not ask for agreement, and does not presuppose ongoing engagement. By the B80 operational principle of Chapter 5, adoption will not spread naturally under such a structure. This is a design choice, not a publicity failure.

To the question of whether this is the publicization of a personal philosophy, the accurate answer is: half yes, half no. That ALIS began from an individual is not denied. As long as the form of the publicization is verification rather than proselytization, however, this is more accurately described as "the verificatory publication of a structure that started from an individual" than as "the publicization of a personal philosophy."

A structure that one individual has built is returned as difference, through the mirrors of others, and in that process it turns into something that is no longer simply of that individual — this is what is taking place in the publication of ALIS. Along the way, the reader, too, participates as one of the mirrors.

## On Misreadings

The six misreadings treated here are all ones that can structurally arise. They do not arise from the reader's carelessness or malice, but from the form that ALIS itself carries.

That it can be taken for relativism stems from the fact that the posture of preserving Δ shares outward features with relativism. That it can be taken for a philosophy that never decides stems from the fact that Polyphonic Wave carries a structure different from simple decisionism. That it can be taken for anti-AI stems from the fact that the boundary design shares outward features with regulatory arguments. That it can be taken for religion stems from the fact that the proprietary vocabulary and normative elements carry a religious appearance. That it can be taken for something merely difficult stems from the fact that proprietary vocabulary and formulas are used for the sake of structural concision. That it can be taken for the publicization of a personal philosophy stems from the fact that it really did start from an individual.

These outward features are also another way of expressing that ALIS takes a particular position. When one takes a position, there exists something else that looks similar to that position. That misreadings arise from surface resemblance is itself evidence that ALIS holds some position at all — what holds no position resembles nothing.

Misreadings cannot be entirely prevented. Nor should one try to prevent them entirely. Accepting that ALIS has a form that can be misread, and responding from the side of those misreadings — that is where this chapter stands. What could be picked up here is only the predictable range of misreadings. Unpredictable misreadings will be returned, in other forms, by future readers.

The next chapter closes, once, the entirety of what this first volume has set out.
