# Chapter 1: Why This Structure Is Needed Now — Setting the Problem

---

There are moments in anyone's daily life when the urge to hurry toward an answer sets in.

Three proposals sit on the table at a meeting, and a judgment about which is correct has to be made by the end of the day. A discussion splits into two currents, and without choosing one the conversation cannot move forward. You want to post something short on social media, but what you want to say cannot be compressed into that length. You don't actually need to decide, yet it feels as though not deciding isn't permitted, and you force yourself to pick one side.

What is happening in these moments?

A state where no answer has emerged, a state where contradiction remains, a state where multiple viewpoints stand side by side — a disposition that treats such states as "failure" is at work in the field. The sense that something remaining undecided must be due to someone's insufficient ability, and that enough effort should resolve it, is embedded in the field.

This sense is not necessarily something anyone consciously brought in. It is simply what drifts through the field.

---

## Fields That Hurry Toward Answers

Within organizations, this disposition operates with particular force.

At the end of a meeting, a "summary" is expected. Three proposals are narrowed to one, and dissenting voices — unless explicitly recorded as "opposition" — are processed as "no objection." The silence of participants who have not actually agreed is entered into the record as agreement. The same phenomenon recurs, in altered forms, in settings where project direction is decided, in executive meetings, and in one-on-one conversations within teams.

In such fields, the question "which one is actually correct" is raised often. The question itself is natural. To act, one must at some point choose one; to choose, one needs a judgment about what is correct.

But when this question is continuously and repeatedly applied to a single field, a particular kind of pressure emerges. In a field where "which is correct" must always be decided, the multiple interpretations that had stood side by side are made to converge on one within a short time. Contradictions that resist convergence are either discarded on one side, or folded into a vague conclusion that appears to contain both.

---

This pressure is not confined to organizational fields.

Consider the situation of expressing an opinion on social media. When trying to say something within a limited character count, only one clearly defined side of one's thinking gets carved out. The parts where one is still uncertain, the parts not yet decided, the complexity of holding two opposing feelings at once — these do not fit within that brevity.

In time, it becomes easier to feel that the short fragment one has carved out is the whole of one's thinking. Thought that was actually more complex, more uncertain, takes on a single clearly outlined shape that conforms to the format of publication. This sharpness of outline, in exchange for being easier for others to receive, ends up flattening the multiple layers that had existed within oneself.

This phenomenon is not the result of an individual making a poor judgment. In fields built for brief communication, multiple layers cannot, structurally, be handled at once. The format itself does not permit plurality.

---

In this way, the premise that "there must be a single answer somewhere" is written into the field again and again. If there must be one answer, then a state in which multiple answers stand side by side is merely a transitional stage. A transitional stage is something that should eventually come to an end, and bringing it to an end counts as progress.

Opportunities to question the premise itself are few.

---

## When AI Enters the Field

The structure of the fields described so far existed before the arrival of AI. Convergence on a single answer within organizations, the simplification of the self on social media, the premise that an answer must exist — all of these are persistent features of human society, not problems that first arose with AI.

However, when AI enters such a field, the tendencies already present are accelerated.

In a meeting, AI recommends a particular proposal. The recommendation comes with the results of data aggregation, comparison, and evaluation. Processing that would take humans several hours is returned in a few seconds. When the speed and volume of processing reach a scale beyond human reach, the impulse to "verify from a different angle" weakens rapidly.

Situations in which a discussion short-circuits with the phrase "if that's what the AI says" increase. This is not because the AI's output is always correct. It is because, on the human side, of the effort required to verify and the effort required to follow the AI's output, the latter has become overwhelmingly lighter. Within the field, the effort of verification comes to be perceived as a cost.

---

Something similar occurs in dialogue with AI.

One throws out what one is thinking to the AI and receives a response. The response comes back in a form aligned with one's question. The more precisely the response aligns, the more it feels like an extension of one's own thinking. A sense of "it understands me" or "what I wanted to say has been put into words" arises.

This sense carries with it the impression that the AI is accurately reflecting one's thinking. Over time, a disposition that treats AI as "a tool for processing one's thoughts externally" takes hold in the user. The feeling strengthens that AI is an extension of oneself, something that lies along the continuation of one's own intent rather than outside it.

What is happening here is not a matter of the precision of the response. It is that the boundary between oneself and the AI grows blurred within the user's own perception. Once the boundary blurs, it becomes difficult to distinguish what the AI returned from what one had originally been thinking. Verifying the AI's output becomes the same as verifying one's own thinking. And doubting one's own thinking was already a difficult task to begin with.

---

The same structure operates at the level of everyday life as well.

Videos, music, news, books, products — recommendation systems learn the user's history and preferences and present what the user is most likely to engage with next. As the precision of recommendations rises, the time spent engaging with what is presented grows longer. The longer the engagement, the more refined the recommendations become on that basis.

Within this cycle, the range of information and expression the user encounters narrows along a particular metric. The metric was built in when the recommendation system was designed; the user did not set it. Believing they are choosing, users go on choosing from what has already been arranged to be easy to choose.

---

And precisely in the period in which this tendency is at work, the capabilities of AI are expanding greatly over a short span of time.

Tasks that AI could not handle a few years ago, it can handle now. A few years from now, it will be able to handle tasks it cannot handle today — voices predicting as much rise from both the technological and the social sides. Whether this expansion is good or bad in itself is not taken up here. The issue rests on a single point: the speed of capability expansion outpaces the adjustment of human dispositions.

The pressure toward convergence on a single answer, which existed before AI, is accelerated by AI's expanding capabilities. The situation in which "following is lighter than verifying" only deepens as AI becomes able to process more.

---

## What Convergence on a Single Answer Takes from the Field

Consider one concrete scene.

In a certain meeting, three proposals were presented, and after discussion, one was adopted. The other two were dismissed. The adopted proposal went on to drive the project forward and produced results. In the memories of those present, the two dismissed proposals gradually fade. They may remain in the records, but they are not referenced in the course of daily operations.

Here, let us attend to one thing.

The two dismissed proposals, beyond simply not having been adopted, **grow increasingly difficult to see as having existed in the field at all**. Whenever it is looked back upon, the adopted proposal is treated as "the one that was chosen at that time." But information about "from among what it was chosen" fades with the passage of time. What remains is only one proposal and its result.

This phenomenon means that something is being lost from the field.

---

What is being lost is not merely "the proposals that were not chosen."

The fact that the field contained multiple interpretations, the fact that multiple directions could have run in parallel, the recognition that the current solution is one among several possibilities and could have been otherwise — these fade from the field.

As a result of this fading, when the next similar situation arrives, the premise that "there must be a single answer" is repeated there as well. Because the memory of multiple things standing side by side does not remain in the field, opportunities to accumulate the experience of multiple things standing side by side grow scarce.

From a single field, over time, movement is lost. By movement is meant the possibility of multiple phases running in parallel, influencing one another, and from there giving rise to other solutions. When convergence on a single solution is repeated, this possibility itself grows thin. The soil of reproduction — the process by which new solutions emerge from the field — gradually grows lean.

---

That it has grown lean is difficult to notice.

Phases that have vanished are not recognized as having vanished. Without any trace of their having been there, they come to be as though they never were. The participants in the field feel that they had been in a field where there was, from the start, only one answer. That way of feeling is accepted as a fair recognition of the field.

The premise that "there must be an answer" is thus self-reinforcing. Because there must be an answer, one narrows it to a single answer. As a result of the narrowing, the traces of multiple things standing side by side disappear. Once the traces disappear, the answer appears to have been one from the start. When it so appears, the premise that "there must be an answer" seems confirmed.

This cycle proceeds independently of the intentions of the individuals participating in the field. It is not that anyone is operating it with malice. It is simply that the structure of the field moves that way.

---

Within this structure, AI readily takes on a particular role.

AI is well suited to referencing vast information and returning the "most appropriate" one. The expression "most appropriate" itself presupposes the existence of a single answer. When AI is introduced into a field where multiple phases are running in parallel, it processes that field as "a field in which options to be chosen are lined up." As a result of that processing, one is chosen and the rest are dismissed.

This is not to say that AI is badly designed. AI responds according to the format in which the human side poses its questions — and when the format is "choose one," AI responds in kind. If the format of the field changes, the response changes as well.

However, as things stand, most of the questions put to AI from the human side take the form of "choose one." When this format is repeated, the speed at which multiple phases disappear from the field becomes faster than in the era when operations were carried out by humans alone.

---

## A Direction That Preserves a Field Where Other Solutions Remain Possible

Up to this point, we have looked at what disappears from the field.

The direction for reclaiming what is disappearing is not "to turn to the side that provides correct answers." Even if one presents a single answer of higher precision, as long as that answer is "a single answer," the structure of the field does not change. Only the quality of the chosen answer improves; the movement by which unchosen phases disappear from the field remains as it is.

Another direction is possible.

A direction that treats the state in which multiple phases run in parallel not as "a transitional stage" or "unresolved" but as the normal state of the field. A direction that acknowledges the simultaneous existence of multiple interpretations not as an incompleteness that must eventually be consolidated into one, but as a richness the field carries.

This direction does not amount to refusing to decide. People need to act, and to act, they need to choose at some point. It is only that, when one chooses, rather than erasing the unchosen from the field as "wrong," one can leave them in the field as "another phase." The phases thus preserved become the soil in which other solutions may be born in the next scene.

---

The chapters that follow present one way of ordering this direction.

The ordering is not an answer. Nor is it a specific remedy for halting the convergence on single answers that is taking place in the present field. It is, as a matter of design, simply an auxiliary line — one drawn to describe the state of the field more precisely and to keep moving while holding multiple phases at once.

An auxiliary line remains invisible unless it is drawn, but drawing it does not automatically resolve anything. The user draws it on their own field and confirms what becomes visible as a result. How to handle what becomes visible belongs to the user's own domain.

In the chapters that follow, the ways of drawing this auxiliary line will be set out one by one.
